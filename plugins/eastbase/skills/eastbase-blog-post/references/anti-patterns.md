# Reference: Anti-patterns (the AI/SaaS writing tells)

> **These are the specific patterns that make content read as generic, AI-generated, or
> sales-y** — each with a concrete **before → after** so "not generic" isn't just a vibe.
> When reviewing a post, quote the offending line and show the rewrite. When writing, if a
> sentence resembles a *before*, it's not done.

## 1. The throat-clearing intro

Generic posts warm up for three sentences before saying anything.

- **Before:** "In today's fast-paced digital landscape, developers are constantly looking
  for ways to streamline their workflow and boost productivity."
- **After:** "I lost an afternoon last week to a caching bug that only showed up in
  production. Here's what it taught me about our deploy setup."

Start at the moment. Delete any intro that would fit on a hundred other blogs.

## 2. Hype adjectives doing the work

If the enthusiasm is in the adjectives instead of the facts, it's hollow.

- **Before:** "Our game-changing, revolutionary tool offers a seamless experience that will
  supercharge your workflow."
- **After:** "It watches your OpenAI spend and pings you when a single day is on track to
  double your usual bill. That's the whole thing."

Cut "game-changing / revolutionary / seamless / supercharge / effortless / unlock your
potential." State what it does; let the reader decide if it's impressive.

## 3. Invented authority and data

Fake specificity is worse than none.

- **Before:** "Studies show that 73% of developers waste 4 hours a week on manual reviews."
- **After:** "I don't have numbers on how common this is — but on our team it was a real
  chunk of every Friday, enough that I built something about it."

If you don't have the data, say what you actually saw. Never manufacture a statistic, a
benchmark, or a customer quote.

## 4. The symmetrical hype tricolon

- **Before:** "It's fast. It's simple. It's powerful."
- **After:** "It's a single screen. You paste a URL, you get a week of posts, you edit the
  three that are wrong."

Concrete beats rhythmic. The tricolon is a tell precisely because it sounds like everything
and describes nothing.

## 5. The aggressive CTA

- **Before:** "Ready to transform your workflow? Sign up today and unlock your full
  potential! 🚀"
- **After:** "If you're monitoring AI spend by refreshing a billing dashboard, this might
  save you the tab. Either way, the two checks above are worth stealing."

Close soft. Offer the takeaway first; the product is a P.S., not the point.

## 6. The listicle with no substance

Ten shallow tips signal that the writer had a headline and no story.

- **Before:** "10 Ways to Improve Your SaaS Onboarding (#7 will surprise you)."
- **After:** "We rewrote our onboarding after watching three people miss the same button.
  Here's the one change that fixed it and the two that didn't."

Prefer one real story with a lesson over a list padded to a round number.

## 7. "We all know" / "As we know" filler

- **Before:** "As we all know, good UX is important for retention. That's why we redesigned
  our onboarding."
- **After:** "We redesigned onboarding after three new users in a row missed the same
  button. Two told us they'd assumed the app was broken."

The truism adds nothing the reader didn't already believe — cut it and lead with the
specific thing that actually happened.

## 8. Over-polished to corporate nothing

When rough founder notes get "improved," the point often dies.

- **Before (polished away):** "At Eastbase, we're committed to delivering exceptional
  developer experiences through thoughtful, user-centric design."
- **After (POV preserved):** "I hate settings pages that make me hunt, so ours has exactly
  the four things people actually change and nothing else. We'll add more when someone asks."

Keep the opinion, the "I," and the edge. Polished and generic is worse than rough and real.

## 9. Pretending a small thing is a universal law

- **Before:** "This is the secret every successful founder knows."
- **After:** "This worked for one small paid tool with a few hundred users. It might not
  scale, and it might be wrong for you — but here's the reasoning."

Generalize carefully. "Here's what I saw" earns more trust than "here's the truth."

## 10. The engagement-bait hook (esp. LinkedIn/X)

- **Before:** "Unpopular opinion: most devs are doing this wrong. 🧵👇 Agree?"
- **After:** "I changed how we do code review last month. One part worked, one part
  annoyed everyone. Notes:"

No manufactured controversy, no "agree?" bait, no thread padding. Say the real thing.
